Description of the Project:

This project involved an in-depth analysis of a 10-year meteorological dataset (weatherHistory.csv) to examine trends related to global warming, specifically focusing on the relationship between apparent temperature and humidity. The dataset contained several key variables, including temperature, apparent temperature, humidity, wind speed, and more, recorded monthly across a 10-year period. The central aim of the project was to test whether the apparent temperature and humidity data, when compared monthly, indicated an increase due to global warming. To test this, two hypotheses were formed: the null hypothesis (H0), which posited that both apparent temperature and humidity showed a consistent increase due to global warming, and the alternative hypothesis (H1), which suggested that these variables did not indicate such an increase.

The analysis revealed an interesting observation â€” a potential negative linear relationship between apparent temperature and humidity, indicating that as the apparent temperature fluctuated, the humidity remained relatively constant over the 10 years. Specifically, humidity remained stable throughout the entire period, while apparent temperature showed frequent and significant changes. These findings suggested that although global warming may have caused noticeable temperature fluctuations, it did not result in a consistent increase in both apparent temperature and humidity.

From the data, it became clear that global warming had contributed to uncertainties in temperature patterns, but not in humidity levels, which remained more stable. This led to the conclusion that, despite temperature irregularities, humidity is a more reliable feature for understanding long-term weather trends in this particular dataset. Overall, the project highlighted the complexity of analyzing weather data and offered insights into how global warming may be influencing temperature trends over extended periods, while leaving humidity largely unchanged.
